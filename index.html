About

Why earth observation matters

I grew up with Hong Kong's defining geotechnical reality: a dense city pressed against steep natural hillsides. Around 30% of the territory has slopes steeper than 30°, roads and buildings sit directly below natural terrain, and the Geotechnical Engineering Office receives on the order of 300 landslide reports a year. Slope safety here is not an abstraction — it is public safety.

That context shaped both halves of my career. As an Assistant Geologist at Ove Arup & Partners Hong Kong since 2021, I have worked on geological risk assessment for new rail lines, site investigation for one of the city's busiest road tunnels, and day-to-day supervision of ground investigation works — logging core, verifying rockhead, and occasionally catching problems no model would see. As a geospatial data scientist (MSc with Distinction, Land Surveying & Geo-Informatics, PolyU), I turn that domain knowledge into deep learning systems that map hazard-relevant ground features at territory scale.

The combination matters globally. Climate-driven extremes are making rockfall, landslides, floods and wildfires more frequent everywhere — and the earth-observation models that forecast them are only as good as the ground truth beneath them. I want to build that next generation of geology-literate geospatial AI, and I'm open to international opportunities in research labs and engineering teams working on Earth observation and natural-hazard forecasting.

Deep learning · MSc dissertation (flagship)

Rock Outcrop Recognition in Hong Kong with Deep Learning on Very High-Resolution Aerial Imagery

PolyU MSc dissertation · Supervisors: Sr Prof. Charles Man Sing Wong & Dr Coco Yin Tung Kwok · Extends Kwok et al. (2018)

Problem

Mapping rock outcrops and boulders in Hong Kong traditionally relies on aerial photograph interpretation and field mapping — subjective, slow, and inconsistent between interpreters. The stakes are high: about 30% of the territory has slopes steeper than 30° (63% steeper than 15°), buildings sit directly against natural hillsides, and roughly 300 landslides are reported to the Geotechnical Engineering Office each year. Knowing where exposed rock and mobile boulders are is a precondition for rockfall hazard assessment — especially as extreme weather intensifies.

Data

  • Imagery: Territory-wide very-high-resolution aerial orthophoto mosaic (0.15 m, 2016–2020), cross-checked against 1963 aerial photography where vegetation now hides boulders.
  • Labels: 4,240 hand-labelled samples (2,112 bedrock, 2,128 boulder) on 256×256 px tiles across Lamma Island, Lantau, Tuen Mun and Po Toi Island, annotated in LabelMe with GEO boulder-inventory maps as reference.
  • Field verification: Site inspections at Shek Pai Wan (Lamma Island) and Hung Shek Mun (NE New Territories) to ground-truth the labels, including finding a >2 m boulder fully hidden by tree canopy in the imagery.

Method

Semantic segmentation benchmark: U-Net and DeepLabV3+ as baselines, then transfer learning with fine-tuned ResNet50V2, InceptionV3 and VGG19 backbones. Pipeline: tiling → normalisation → mask binarisation → data augmentation (flips, rotation, shifts, zoom, noise) → 70/15/15 train/validation/test split → staged layer unfreezing and hyperparameter search (epochs, learning rate, dropout, augmentation policy).

Test accuracy after hyperparameter and augmentation tuning. Baselines without fine-tuning could not separate bedrock from boulders despite >80% training accuracy; fine-tuned backbones generalised to untrained regions (ResNet50V2 reached 90.0% training accuracy).

Beyond the metrics: the work exposed real data-quality lessons — vegetation and moss camouflage boulders in imagery, boundary ambiguity biases labels, and multi-epoch imagery (1963 vs 2021) is needed where canopy has closed. These findings shape how the model should be used: as a fast screening layer feeding geotechnical follow-up, not a replacement for it.

Remote sensing · Google Earth Engine

Quantifying a Wildfire's Impact on Air Quality from Space — Quebec, June 2023

PolyU MSc · remote sensing group project

Problem

2023 was Canada's worst wildfire year on record — 3,000+ fires and over 80,000 km² burned. We asked a measurable question: for one major fire in Baie-James, Quebec (perimeter 1,229.8 km², 11 June – 10 July 2023), how much vegetation burned, and how much did air quality degrade before, during and after?

Data & method

  • Burned-area mapping: 3 m Planet imagery pre/post fire; NDVI, SAVI and Burned Area Index with severity thresholds cross-checked across all three indices.
  • Air quality time series: Sentinel-5P carbon monoxide column density and UV Aerosol Index, April–September, over the fire perimeter plus a 10 km buffer, processed in Google Earth Engine.
  • Meteorological drivers: GSMaP hourly rainfall and MODIS land surface temperature, 2017–2023, with correlation testing.
Google Earth EnginePlanet (3 m)Sentinel-5P MODIS LSTGSMaPNDVI / SAVI / BAI

Results

~800 km²vegetation affected by the fire (NDVI change)
556–563 km²high-severity burn (NDVI & SAVI agree closely)
+0.029mol/m² rise in mean CO column density in the fire month
+0.55jump in UV Aerosol Index vs the previous month

CO and aerosol loading spiked sharply in June and recovered by September, giving satellite-only evidence of the fire's air-quality footprint. Regional context: 2023 rainfall was 43% below 2017 levels while land surface temperature trended upward — the dry, hot setup that primes boreal fire seasons.

My contributions included the spectral index thresholding analysis and the critical evaluation of threshold transferability between climate regions — the study is explicit that index thresholds are study-area-specific and quick-look results need ground truthing.

Spatial analysis · GIS

Flood Risk Assessment for Hong Kong with GIS Hydrological Modelling

PolyU MSc · advanced GIS group project · I led Results & Data Analysis

Problem

Hong Kong averages ~2,400 mm of rain a year, and single events can be catastrophic — Typhoon Haikui's remnant dropped 641 mm in one night in September 2023, flooding 60+ locations and causing over HK$100 million in losses. Official drainage blackspots tell you where flooding has been reported; we wanted a terrain-first model of where flood risk actually concentrates.

Data & method

  • Terrain: Territory-wide 5 m LiDAR-derived Digital Terrain Model (Lands Department).
  • Land use: Planning Department "Land Utilization in Hong Kong" 10 m raster (2023), ranked by water-holding capacity.
  • Rainfall: 14 HKO automatic weather stations for three contrasting typhoons (Hato 2017, Mangkhut 2018, Yagi 2024), interpolated with IDW.
  • Hydrology workflow (ArcGIS): Sink filling → D8 flow direction → flow accumulation → runoff thresholding → stream linking and Strahler ordering → watershed delineation → inundation analysis overlaid on land use, benchmarked against Drainage Services Department flooding blackspots.
ArcGIS hydrology5 m LiDAR DTMIDW interpolation D8 flow modellingStrahler orderingWatershed delineation

Results

Across all three typhoon scenarios, flood-risk hotspots recur in Sha Tin, Tai Po and Tsuen Wan — inland districts, not just the coastal strip — plus Sham Shui Po in Kowloon and Tin Shui Wai in the northwest. Risk did not track simple proximity to the sea or any single land-use class, and several modelled hotspots are not captured by official blackspots — an argument for terrain-based screening alongside incident records.

Urban big data · GIS + ML

Which Hong Kong District Has the Highest Rooftop Solar Potential?

PolyU MSc · urban & geospatial big-data analytics group project

Problem

Hong Kong has decarbonisation targets and a feed-in tariff scheme, but no consolidated picture of how much rooftop photovoltaic potential the city actually has — or which districts and building types would deliver the most.

Data & method

  • Potential mapping: Territory-wide building footprints filtered by rooftop suitability criteria, with per-district electricity production estimates by building category.
  • Existing installations: A pre-trained machine-learning model applied to satellite imagery to detect rooftops that already have solar panels across all 18 districts.
  • Economics: Generation value estimated under the feed-in tariff scheme.
GIS spatial analysisBuilding footprints ML image classificationFeed-in tariff modelling

Results

Yuen Long ranked highest in rooftop PV potential, with residential buildings the main contributor territory-wide; the dense northern districts of Hong Kong Island ranked lowest. Detection of existing installations showed that even in the top districts, under 2% of identified rooftops currently host panels — the potential is overwhelmingly untapped.

Mobility analytics · Spatial big data

Urban Mobility Analytics — Bike-Share and Taxi Flows in Chicago and New York

PolyU MSc · urban & geospatial big-data analytics

Problem

City-scale trip records are noisy and huge; the analytical job is turning millions of origin–destination points into legible spatial demand patterns that planners can act on.

Data & method

  • Chicago bike-share: A single-day snapshot of ~20,000 trips across 535 stations and 3,800+ bikes, with departure and arrival mapping, trip-duration distributions and station-level demand.
  • New York taxis: Yellow-taxi trips aggregated over Manhattan taxi zones to map departure intensity across the street grid.
Trip-record processingSpatial aggregation ArcGIS cartographyDemand analysis

Results

Bike-share demand concentrates sharply along Chicago's lakefront and downtown loop, while outlying stations see single-digit daily trips — a classic hub-and-tail distribution that matters for rebalancing. Manhattan taxi departures track the avenue grid with intense clustering in Midtown and Lower Manhattan.

3D GIS · Technical review

3D GIS Modelling, Analysis & Applications

PolyU MSc · advanced GIS individual assignment

Scope

A technical review of how GIS moves from flat maps to volumetric models: what separates 2D, 2.5D and true 3D GIS, how 3D data is captured, and where the technology is heading.

Topics covered

  • Data models: 2D vector/raster → 2.5D height fields (TIN, raster surfaces, DEMs) → true 3D voxels, meshes and CityGML, and what each enables, from slope and viewshed analysis to subsurface geological modelling and BIM integration.
  • Capture technologies: Airborne, terrestrial and mobile LiDAR versus UAV photogrammetry, comparing accuracy, range, cost and processing trade-offs.
  • Trends: Drone-mounted LiDAR, vehicle- and train-mounted real-time scanning, and the value of Hong Kong's open territory-wide LiDAR data (the same 0.15 m-class data underpinning my dissertation and the 5 m DTM used in the flood study).
